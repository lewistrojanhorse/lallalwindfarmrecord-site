Repository Methodology

Understanding the Record

This page explains how to read the Lal Lal Wind Farm Record: the documentary hierarchy, the verification method, the role of Freedom of Information material, and the way each evidence page should be approached.

Reading position: The Glossary explains technical terms. This page explains the method. It does not repeat definitions that now belong in the Glossary.
Purpose

Why understanding the record matters

The repository is not arranged as a general commentary page. It is arranged so an independent reader can move from context, to terms, to source documents, and then to the evidentiary issue pages.

1

Context first

Start Here introduces the Lal Lal Wind Farm, the project chronology and the basic pathway from planning approval to this repository.

2

Terms once

The Glossary is the learning hub. Technical, planning and administrative terms are explained there first, then linked throughout the site.

3

Evidence by issue

Evidence pages such as K15aa and SAC then apply the same method to particular documentary questions.

Method

How evidence pages should be read

Each evidentiary page is intended to separate framework, disclosed documents and administrative observation.

A

Requirement

The page identifies the relevant permit, standard, testing-plan or regulatory requirement as the starting point.

B

Disclosed record

The page then records what has been disclosed through reports, correspondence, agency records or Freedom of Information processes.

C

Verification question

The page asks whether an independent reader can reconcile the requirement with the documents presently available.

Important: This repository does not determine compliance, breach, liability, legal rights or regulatory findings. It records the documentary pathway and whether the disclosed record enables independent verification.
Verification

Evidence before opinion

The repository is built around documentary verification. That means the site prefers primary documents, dated correspondence, disclosed records and formal decisions over unsupported assertion.

What counts as useful record material?

Useful material includes planning permits, endorsed plans, technical reports, monitoring documents, agency correspondence, public meeting records, FOI decisions, disclosed emails, auditor material and Trustee records.

Where the record is incomplete, the site should say so in restrained terms rather than filling the gap with speculation.

What the site avoids

The repository avoids substituting its own findings for a regulator, court, auditor or technical decision-maker. Its function is to preserve, organise and explain the record so readers can inspect the material directly.

FOI and custody

Why Freedom of Information matters

Freedom of Information material is important because it shows what records agencies have disclosed, what records they identify as held, and where the disclosed record does or does not contain a particular document.

Disclosed does not always mean complete

A disclosed record is the material available for examination at a particular point in time. It may include released documents, partial releases, refusal reasons, searches, correspondence and agency decision letters.

How FOI material is used

FOI material is used as a custody and verification tool. It helps identify whether the documentary pathway can be followed from the planning framework through technical assessment and regulatory reliance.
