Plain English Guide

Understanding the Record

A plain English guide to the terms, organisations and documents used throughout the Lal Lal Wind Farm administrative record.

Lal Lal Wind Farm entrance sign. Image included as contextual orientation for readers.

Why this page exists

This website contains planning, technical and regulatory documents. Many of those documents use specialised language used by planners, acousticians, regulators and lawyers.

This guide explains the most common terms in everyday language. You do not need any planning, engineering, legal or acoustic experience to understand the information below.

Simple rule: read this page first, then move into the detailed evidence.

The Lal Lal Wind Farm

The Lal Lal Wind Farm is a wind energy facility located near Elaine and Yendon in western Victoria.

It consists of 60 wind turbines and has an installed capacity of approximately 228 megawatts.

This website is not about whether wind energy is good or bad. It documents the administrative, regulatory and evidentiary record relating to the planning, construction, operation and regulation of the Lal Lal Wind Farm.

Operator informationinfo@lallalwindfarm.com.au

Government organisations

These are the main organisations that appear throughout this repository.

Department of Transport and Planning (DTP)

What is it? DTP is the Victorian Government department connected with Victoria's planning system.

Why is it relevant? The Lal Lal Wind Farm operates under a Planning Permit issued through the Victorian planning system. DTP appears throughout this record in relation to planning permits, amendments, correspondence, compliance records and FOI decisions.

Examples: Planning Permit, NCTP, permit amendments, FOI decisions and planning correspondence.

Official websiteEmail DTP

Moorabool Shire Council

What is it? Moorabool Shire Council is the local government authority for much of the area in which the Lal Lal Wind Farm is located.

Why is it relevant? Historically, Council was the Responsible Authority responsible for administering and enforcing the Planning Permit. Many earlier planning, compliance, complaint and Community Reference Group records originated from, or were held by, Council.

Examples: Council correspondence, CRG minutes, FOI responses and historical compliance material.

Council wind farm pageEmail Council

Environment Protection Authority Victoria (EPA)

What is it? EPA is Victoria's environmental regulator.

Why is it relevant? EPA regulates pollution and administers environmental laws. For operational wind farms, EPA has an important role in the regulation of wind turbine noise, including under the Environment Protection Regulations.

Examples: Pollution reports, EPA correspondence, FOI decisions, auditor records, Noise Management Plan material and operational regulatory records.

Official websiteEmail EPA

Australian Energy Infrastructure Commissioner (AEIC)

What is it? The AEIC helps communities, landholders and renewable energy developers resolve concerns relating to renewable energy projects.

Why is it relevant? The AEIC provides an independent pathway for community engagement and complaint resolution. It does not administer the Planning Permit or regulate environmental compliance.

Examples: Complaint correspondence, community engagement material, acknowledgements and guidance.

Official websiteEmail AEIC

Common terms explained

Each explanation is deliberately short. The purpose is to make the detailed record easier to read.

Dwelling Plain English

A dwelling is simply a house where people live. In wind farm noise reports, a dwelling may also be described as a noise-sensitive receiver because it is a place where noise may be measured or assessed.

Noise-sensitive receiver

A noise-sensitive receiver is a place where people live or regularly spend time, and where noise could affect them. Technical reports often identify each receiver using a code instead of repeatedly writing the full street address.

K15aa and other location codes

K15aa is not the name of a house or a person. It is the identification code used in technical reports for one particular dwelling used as a reference location for measuring and assessing wind farm noise.

Other dwellings and locations have their own identifiers, such as H18aa, L18aa, J14aa and many others. Think of these identifiers like reference numbers on a map. They help reports consistently refer to the same place without repeatedly using the full street address.

These identifiers are reference labels. They do not, by themselves, indicate ownership, importance or priority.

Background sound

Before the wind farm is constructed and the turbines are installed, the existing sound around nearby homes is measured. These measurements are called background sound.

They provide a starting point so that, after the wind farm begins operating, new measurements can be compared with the sounds that already existed.

Baseline report

A baseline report records background sound measurements before normal operation of the wind farm. It provides the reference point against which later operational noise measurements can be compared.

Commissioning

Commissioning is the period after construction has finished when the wind turbines are tested before entering normal operation. During commissioning, equipment is checked, adjusted and tested to ensure it operates correctly.

Compliance monitoring

Compliance monitoring means checking whether the wind farm is operating within the requirements of the Planning Permit and related approved documents.

Planning Permit

A Planning Permit is the legal approval allowing the wind farm to be built and operated. It also contains conditions that must be complied with throughout the life of the project.

Planning Permit condition

Think of the Planning Permit as a licence. The conditions are the rules attached to that licence.

Responsible Authority

The Responsible Authority is the government body responsible for administering and enforcing the Planning Permit. The identity of the Responsible Authority can change over time.

NZS 6808:2010

NZS 6808:2010 is the Standard used to assess wind farm noise under the relevant planning framework. It deals with background sound, operational noise assessment and compliance methods.

Copyright note: this website refers to NZS 6808:2010 for context and terminology, but does not reproduce the Standard.

Noise Compliance Test Plan (NCTP)

The Noise Compliance Test Plan explains how the wind farm must demonstrate compliance with the Planning Permit. It describes where sound is measured, how measurements are taken, when monitoring occurs and what information must be reported.

Attended listening observations

A microphone can measure how loud a sound is. A trained acoustician can also describe what the sound actually sounds like.

An attended listening observation occurs when an acoustician visits a location, listens while measurements are being taken, records what they hear and compares those observations with instrument readings.

Under the Lal Lal Wind Farm NCTP, attended observations also included informative observations close to the nearest turbines during at least one set of observations. This helps determine whether sounds heard at a dwelling are also audible near the turbines.

During a public information session, SLR's Gustaf Royard explained that listening closer to turbines can help confirm whether a sound heard in the distance is actually coming from those turbines. He also described the listening test as the "Gateway" to further analysis.

Special Audible Characteristics (SAC)

Sometimes wind turbine noise has characteristics that make it more noticeable than ordinary wind noise. Examples include tones, impulses, rhythmic beating, modulation or other distinctive sound qualities. These are called Special Audible Characteristics, often shortened to SAC.

Environmental Auditor

An Environmental Auditor is an independent professional appointed under Victorian environmental legislation to review particular environmental matters. Some wind farm noise documents are reviewed by an Environmental Auditor before being provided to regulators.

Noise Management Plan

A Noise Management Plan explains how operational wind turbine noise will be managed after the wind farm begins operating.

Freedom of Information (FOI)

A Freedom of Information request is a legal process that allows members of the public to request government documents. Many documents discussed on this website were obtained through FOI processes.

Primary source document

A primary source document is an original document created at the time an event occurred, or by a person or organisation directly involved. Examples include permits, reports, meeting minutes, emails, letters and FOI decisions.

Documentary evidence

Documentary evidence is information contained in documents rather than opinions or recollections. This website places emphasis on documentary evidence so readers can examine the source material directly.

Documentary record

The documentary record is the collection of documents available for examination about a particular issue. It may include reports, letters, emails, meeting minutes, permits, plans, maps, FOI documents and other records.

Administrative record

The administrative record is the documentary record showing how decisions were made, what information was available at the time and how the regulatory process developed.

Disclosed record

The disclosed record means the documents that have actually been released or disclosed at a particular point in time, usually through FOI or another official process.

It does not necessarily mean other documents never existed. It simply means those are the records available for examination at that point in time.

How to read this website

Most pages follow a simple pattern:

1. Plain English

The issue is introduced in everyday language.

2. Evidence

Key documentary evidence is identified and explained.

3. Source documents

Readers can open the underlying primary documents and check the record themselves.

The repository is intended to preserve and explain the administrative record. It does not determine compliance, liability, legal rights or regulatory findings.
