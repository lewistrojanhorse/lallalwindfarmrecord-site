Record Methodology

Independent Documentary Verification

A plain-English explanation of how this record compares mandatory requirements with the disclosed administrative and evidentiary record.

Purpose of this page: Documentary verification does not make a compliance finding. It asks whether an independent reader can follow the requirement, locate the relevant source material, and reconcile that requirement with the disclosed record.
What verification means

Requirement, record, reconciliation

The verification method sits between the governing Framework and the detailed evidence pages. It keeps the record focused on documents rather than opinion.

1

Identify the requirement

The page starts with the relevant Planning Permit, NZS 6808:2010, Noise Compliance Testing Plan or other governing document.

2

Locate the disclosed record

The record then identifies the disclosed reports, correspondence, Freedom of Information material and agency records relevant to that requirement.

3

Ask what can be verified

The final question is limited: does the disclosed record allow the requirement to be independently followed, checked or reconciled?

Important limitation

Verification is not adjudication

This record does not determine compliance, breach, liability, legal rights or regulatory findings.

It records whether the disclosed documents allow a reader to trace the pathway from a mandatory requirement to the documents said to demonstrate, address or rely upon that requirement.

Terminology discipline

Use the record's terms carefully

The record uses the technical and documentary language appearing in the governing framework. Where a term needs explanation, the first-use link should point to the Glossary.

For example, the record uses noise-sensitive location where that is the relevant NZS terminology, rather than substituting a different phrase.

How to read a verification issue

The K15aa method

The K15aa page remains the benchmark for evidentiary presentation. Other issue pages should follow the same restrained structure.

D

Q

What question remains for an independent reader when the requirement is compared with the disclosed record?

Existence-based requests

How “no documents found” outcomes are treated

A decision recording that searches did not locate a responsive document is preserved as evidence of the agency's search and decision outcome. It is not presented as proof that the document never existed. Where a request contains a separate existence-based part that is not expressly determined in the notice, later clarification correspondence is preserved alongside the decision.
